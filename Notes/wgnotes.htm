Super glue (cyanoacrylate)

Super glue Barbara Cashman Joined: 09 Mar 2003 Posts: 543 Location: Greensboro NC New post Posted: Fri Jul 27, 2007 10:02 pm Post subject: Debunking "Super" GlueReply with quote Report Post I have heard the rumor that using super glue (main ingredient cyanoacrylate) releases cyanide gas when used at high fusing temps. I spent some time with the chemists about this ingredient. The bottom line is something like this: It is theoretically possible to release cyanide gas at these temps. What's happening with the glue is that the flash point is under 300 deg. Then it actually re-liquifies and eventually burns off. However, the Health and Safety dept for these companies agree that it would take one hell of a lot of glue to even register.. .given the conditions were true to create the environment in the first place. I told them I typically use 2 whole small tubes of glue for a kiln load. I use it to hold pieces in place for handling. That was no more than 6 grams. They agreed that if there was any danger, it was minimal. I really wanted the inside info on this, as I have had teachers use and teach its use in class, and others that raised the caution flags. I would say to any newbie or any not-so-newbie that you should use your own discretion. However, the "survey says" it's probably not a problem in the quantities that we work with. _________________ Barbara J Cashman www.glastile.com Joined: 19 May 2003 Posts: 1259 Location: Portland, OR New post Posted: Fri Jul 27, 2007 10:34 pm Post subject: Reply with quote Report Post Interesting. SO. What if you froze a piece and then *buried* it in a tub (Stainless or ceramic) full of a sand/silica flour/kiln wash mix. If you really packed the powder around it, it would support the piece until it starts to fuse, after which it would hold together by itself. I think that this might be a way to get more spindly pieces. You'd have to clean the piece up afterwards, but that is nothing new to PDV artists. (By the way, this is how faience articles are glazed using the cementation technique, so there is some precedent here) Of course, you could go one better and *paint the frozen piece with Hydroperm*. Then invert it and voila: you have a refractory mold formed from the glass rather than the other way round! I know it sounds weird. What would be the benefit? Several things: You would know what colors you have before it goes in the kiln. Having an investment layer around the piece, you would be free to go to higher temperatures with no fear of slumping. You would be less likely to get bubbles in the investment. You would still be able to produce a piece with much less effort. Does not know the ratio nor the COE _________________ Paul Kimball Pyros Glass Studio www.pyrosglass.com ******'' ********* Freeze'n'fire Here are a couple of tips for working with flexible molds and frozen frit: 1. We use powder and find that it fuses at lower temperatures and with a better surface finish than the bigger frit. We do use bigger frit in the interior of pieces, though. The finer the frit on the surface of your piece, the better it will come out (assuming you want a shiny surface). We have even tried grinding our own extra fine powder (in a rock tumbler) and adding FuseMaster enamels to the regular powders. Both of these gave really great results. 2. The key to getting this to work is to make sure that the glass paste is really tamped down in the mold. That way your piece holds together even after it dries out and it won't crumble around the edges. What we do is vibrate the mold while adding the glass paste. You can do this easily just by tapping the mold with the edge of a palette knife. The glass slurry settles down and the water rises to the surface. Blot it off with a flat piece of tissue or paper towel. Now tap some more. Don't be too gentle! You really want the glass to settle in. Phil Teefy uses one of those vibrating back thumpers on the table to vibrate his molds. Keep tapping and blotting until you don't get a sheen on top when you tap the mold. This means you have *just enough* water left in the glass to hold it together. 3. Since plastic and rubber molds naturally repel water, we used to have a lot of trouble with bubbles getting caught in the mold, particularly in things like the points of a shell. Then Raphael Schnepf told us about something called Kodak Photo-Flo. It's a wetting agent that is used by photographers. We mix a few drops with the water before we add the glass powder, and it just works like a charm. We put it in the water first, then pour the dry powder or frit into the water. This way the bubbles never get into the slurry in the first place, and the slurry flows beautifully into the molds. 4. After you have frozen your pieces, they are surprisingly durable as long as they stay frozen. I have dropped pieces on the floor, picked them up and put them in the kiln and they came out just fine. However, if you do break one while it is frozen, we have not found any way to put it back together. You just can't push it close enough together to fix the break. You're better off just thawing it out and mixing it up again. 5. This is really important. Put your piece on a warm primed kiln shelf and let it dry for *at least* an hour before firing. We let our good stuff dry overnight. As it dries it will become quite fragile, so don't try to move it around after it thaws. Why do this? Because you want the water in the piece to melt and soak into the kiln shelf. This sucks the piece tighter together and pulls the fine particles down into the surface of the piece. If you don't do this, and fire right away, the water in the piece turns to steam and goes out through the top of the piece. No, it won't explode, but this carries fine particles out to the surface and results in a cloudy layer particularly in the convex parts of the design. At least, that's my theory. I found this out totally by accident. I was really pulling my hair out over the cloudiness and was doing a gazillion tests. Nothing was working and I was losing a lot of sleep. One night about 3 AM I pulled a critter out of the freezer and popped it on the kiln shelf. I was going to fire it, but fell asleep on the sofa by mistake. 12 hours later, refreshed, I fired the piece and it came out PERFECT. 6. Pieces do shrink about 15% in all directions. We use the HotLine Primo Primer since it has less "tooth" than the other primers and lets our pieces shrink uniformly without sticking to the shelf. 7. One of the coolest things about this technique is that you can get AMAZINGLY thin sections. We make pieces that are 1/16" thick and yet have full 3D detail. You can make leaves and flowers and then tack them onto plates, tiles, etc. 8. Since the pieces do shrink, fire them first, before you tack fuse them onto other solid glass pieces. If you try to put a thick frozen frit piece onto a piece of plain glass and fuse it all together, it warps something awful. 9. The bigger the piece, the slower you have to ramp it as you get up to the fusing temperature. For pieces made with COE 90 powder, this temperature is between 1275 and 1300 degrees F. For little pieces you can ramp at 600 degrees/hour right up to 1300 and hold for 20 minutes. For big pieces you will want to creep up at maybe 50 degrees per hour after you pass 1200. Coming down the glass is a solid mass and you should anneal as appropriate for the size of the piece. Ideally, all the glass in the piece reaches the critical temperature at the same time. It's really a wierd thing to watch.. When the piece starts to fuse, it just happens all at once. You can actually watch it shrinking before your eyes, and the interior starts to glow. (Please use IR eye protection if you like to watch. You don't want to get cataracts!). Also, the bigger the piece, the lower the temperature at which you should fuse it, to avoid slumping. Phil Teefy fuses his boxes and bowls at about 1275, I think. He'll also be in Las Vegas; you might want to ask him. 10. This is so totally FUN, and it is a great technique for kids. After the glass powder is mixed with water, it is as totally safe as a craft can get, and there is no binder to burn out or anything. For the upcoming show, we made hundreds of Glow Bunnies. I'm never happier than when tapping a big candy mold full of critters. Razz I Hope this helps! 5. as fast as possible until 532°C, holding 300min (to avoid devitrification and anneal the transparent bullseye glass
6.10°C/h until 501°C, holding 300 min (annealing of the opaque bullseye glass)
7. 10°C/h until 472°C, holding 150 min (annealing of the gold purple bullseye glass)
8. 10°C/h until 350°C
9. as slow as the oven do until room temperature

The reaction of darker colored glass ( like cobalt blue and emerald green) with the investment is what I've said for years, causes cavitation. And that's with leaded crystal. With soda-lime glass, the problem is even worse. Even the clear soda-lime can get "set-off". Its very hard to get cavitation with clear leaded crystal. But this reaction does not cause "bubbles", per se (see below). I've had plenty of castings with very little bubble and lots of cavitation. The reaction develops between the glass and the investment and causes a gas pocket to develop between the cooling glass and the investment and pushes the glass away from the investment wall, so the glass cannot fill the investment form properly. And as the glass gets near softening point, on the way down, the "cavitation gas" can keep the glass away from the investment until the glass starts to solidify, and leave the reminder of this reaction in the glass. Its always ugly. On large pieces you will never get cavitation down low on the form. Cavitation will occur where this gas reaction can overcome the weight of the glass. On smaller forms this could occur almost anywhere. But for the most part, as a rule of thumb, cavitation will occur on the upper portions of the casting, and it likes it near the sprue, where there is the least amount of pressure to overcome from the glass (hydrostatic pressure). But even if you cast the same piece, same color, same way, with a piece prone to cavitation, you would get cavitation in different areas of the different castings each time. I attribute this to the fact of the "cavitating reaction" flaring up in some spot, then gradually dying off, and a new reaction flaring up someplace else, and on and on. You will never get the same cavitation problem in the same place in the same way. Its unpredictable in that way. I've cast the same piece with the same colors and same investment and same schedules, and it was a real puzzler to me dealing with the unpredictability of placement, for a while. The cavitation hold at 50F above softening point, on the way down, holds the glass at a slightly molten state until this "cavitating reaction" slows down greatly or stops, so that the glass can finally fill the mold successfully. That's my theory and it works to get cavitation free (or nearly free) cast glass. I will say the chilling effect of the sprue can accentuate cavitation in soda-lime glass, thus especially for soda-lime castings, I would cover the feed sprue with some type of insulation like fiber ceramics. It will help. Don't look for something that looks like "bubbles" as evidence of cavitation. Could this reaction between the glass and the investment cause bubbles when the glass is more liquid. Who knows? How can you tell? Put a tiny gas-o-meter probe into the bubble and see if it contains atmosphere or cavitation gas? But the ugly depressions, the gashes on the surface of the glass, that's what you have to grind out later. From a glass casting point of view, that's what you want to control or get rid of. A few bubbles under the surface? Eh, what's the worry? Hugh
